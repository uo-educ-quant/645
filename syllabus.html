II. Course Overview

This course is the third in a sequence intended to provide a toolkit of statistical concepts, methods, and their implementation to producers of applied research in education, human services, and other social sciences. The course is organized around the principle that research design depends in part on researchers’ substantive questions and their quantitative data available to address these questions. In this upper-level introductory course, students will learn about and practice using analytic models and strategies that are suitable for examining data that have (a) a dichotomous outcome variable, (b) a count outcome variable, (c) a nested structure, and/or (d) missing values; students will also learn about when and how to estimate (e) internal consistency and interrater reliability for research purposes. The course seeks to blend a conceptual, mathematical, and applied understanding of introductory statistical concepts. At the core of our pedagogical approach is the belief that students learn statistical analysis by doing statistical analysis.

EDUC 643 (or a similar intermediate statistics course) is a pre-requisite as is a basic familiarity with a statistical programming language (preferably R).

III. Student Learning Outcomes

By the end of this term, it is expected that students will be able to:

  1. Determine when it is appropriate to use logistic regression or Poisson regression analyses and analyses that account for nested data in education and human services research.

  2. Conduct and interpret basic logistic and Poisson regression analyses and analyses for nested data.

  3. Describe the assumptions of logistic and Poisson regression analyses and analyses for nested data, and test analytic models for the extent to which they satisfy the models’ assumptions.

  4. Explain and apply the concepts of reliability and agreement in measurement for research purposes.

  5. Explain common mechanisms of missingness and the applications and limitations of different methods for handling missing data.

  6. Assess missing data characteristics and implement multiple imputation to handle missing data.

  7. Perform all analyses using an object-oriented, open-source programming language.

Textbooks

The following text and reading materials are required:

  • Roback, P., & Legler, J. (2021). Beyond multiple linear regression: Applied generalized linear models and multilevel models in R (1st ed.). CRC Press. https://bookdown.org/roback/bookdown-BeyondMLR/

  • Dunn, T. J., Baguley, T., & Brunsden V. (2014). From alpha to omega: A practical solution to the pervasive problem of internal consistency estimation. British Journal of Psychology, 105, 399–412. https://doi.org/10.1111/bjop.12046

  • Little, T. D., Jorgensen, T. D., Lang, K. M., & Moore, W. G. (2013). On the joys of missing data. Journal of Pediatric Psychology, 39(2), 151–162. https://doi.org/10.1093/jpepsy/jst048

Quizzes

Five classes will begin with a very short multiple-choice quiz that is designed to test your knowledge of the theoretical principles underlying the statistics we are studying for the week. Quizzes will be open book and notes.

Assignments

The goal of the assignments is to practice the concepts and vocabulary we have been modeling in class and implement some of the techniques we have learned. Each assignment has an associated data source, short codebook and detailed instructions for the required data and analytic tasks. You may work on your own or with up to two (2) partners. If you choose to work with others, please make sure that you engage in a full, fair, and mutually-agreeable collaboration. Plan, execute, and write-up your analyses together; do not simply divide up the work. Further assignment details are available here. Assignments will be evaluated based on students’ performance in completing the assignment tasks, showing their work, and in demonstrating their understanding of the course concepts and terminology.

Final

The final assignment involves a more extended application and synthesis of the concepts of descriptive and relational applied data analysis covered in this course.
